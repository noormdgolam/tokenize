Tokenized Sports Contracts: Investing in Athlete Earnings

By Tokenize Editorial Team • 2026-07-14

Key Takeaways

  • Athletes can sell security tokens representing a percentage of their future salaries, endorsement deals, or tournament winnings.
  • Fans transition from passive spectators to active financial stakeholders, earning a yield directly tied to the athlete's performance.
  • The model presents massive regulatory hurdles, as these tokens are classified as securities under the SEC's Howey Test.

In 2020, NBA guard Spencer Dinwiddie made financial history by tokenizing a portion of his $34 million guaranteed contract. He issued debt tokens to investors, receiving upfront capital in exchange for paying them back with interest derived from his future NBA paychecks. This event birthed an entirely new asset class: Tokenized Sports Contracts.

The Economics of Athlete Tokenization

For a minor league baseball player or an emerging tennis star, the path to the major leagues is incredibly expensive. Training, travel, and coaching require immense upfront capital. By tokenizing a percentage of their future earnings (an Income Share Agreement), a young athlete can raise funds directly from their fan base.

If the athlete succeeds and signs a massive contract, the smart contract automatically routes the agreed-upon percentage of their salary—converted to stablecoins—directly to the wallets of the token holders.

Tokenized sports contracts represent the ultimate evolution of fantasy sports. A fan holding a token is financially incentivized to buy the athlete's merchandise, watch their games, and boost their social media presence, as increased endorsements directly increase the token's yield.

However, US investors must be aware of the strict legal reality: these tokens are unregistered securities. Unless offered under strict Reg D or Reg A+ exemptions, buying and trading human capital contracts on secondary markets is highly restricted by the SEC.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if the athlete gets injured and cannot play?

Just like investing in a startup, if the underlying asset (the athlete) fails to generate revenue due to injury or poor performance, the token holders receive nothing, and the investment may go to zero.

Has an athlete ever tokenized their contract?

Yes, the most famous early example was NBA player Spencer Dinwiddie, who successfully tokenized a portion of his $34 million contract in 2020.
