Reg A+ vs Reg D: SEC Exemptions for Tokenized Offerings

By Tokenize Editorial Team • 2026-07-14

Key Takeaways

  • Regulation D (Rule 506c) is the fastest way to issue a tokenized asset, but strictly limits sales to verified Accredited Investors.
  • Regulation A+ allows sales to the general public (retail investors) but requires expensive, time-consuming SEC qualification.
  • Tokens issued under Reg D are subject to a mandatory 12-month lock-up period before they can be traded on secondary markets.

Reg A+ vs Reg D: SEC Exemptions for Tokenized Offerings

Reg D: Private Placement Exemption

Reg D is a well-established SEC exemption allowing private companies to raise funds without undergoing a public offering process. The exemption is characterized by a limited number of investors and strict restrictions on advertising and solicitation. To qualify for Reg D, tokenized offerings must satisfy the following conditions:

  • The issuer must be a company or limited liability company (LLC)
  • The offering must be a private placement, meaning it is not publicly marketed or advertised
  • The number of accredited investors must be limited to 35 in a 12-month period, unless the issuer takes reasonable care to ensure that the offering is available to only accredited investors
  • The issuer must verify the accredited investor status of each investor

Reg D offers several benefits, including flexibility in the offering process and the ability to keep sensitive information confidential. However, the exemption also has limitations, such as the strict investor verification requirements and the lack of transparency.

Reg A+: Public Offering Exemption

Reg A+ is a relatively new SEC exemption that provides startups with an affordable and efficient way to raise capital through public offerings. Unlike Reg D, Reg A+ allows for the public solicitation of investments, and there are no restrictions on the number of investors. To qualify for Reg A+, tokenized offerings must satisfy the following conditions:

  • The issuer must be a company or LLC
  • The offering must be a public offering
  • The issuer must file an offering statement with the SEC, which includes financial statements and other information about the company
  • The issuer must file periodic reports with the SEC during the offering period

Reg A+ offers several benefits, including the ability to raise significant amounts of capital and the requirement for transparency, which enhances investor confidence. However, the exemption also has limitations, such as the need to file with the SEC and the public disclosure of sensitive information.

Comparing Reg A+ and Reg D

The choice between Reg A+ and Reg D ultimately depends on the issuer's specific needs and goals. Reg D provides more flexibility and control over the offering process but comes with stricter investor verification requirements and a lack of transparency. Reg A+, on the other hand, allows for public solicitation of investments and transparency but requires more extensive disclosure and reporting obligations.

In the context of tokenized offerings, Reg A+ may be a more suitable choice for businesses seeking to raise significant amounts of capital and build credibility through transparency. However, Reg D may be a better fit for startups requiring more control over the offering process and a limited number of investors.

As the tokenized offerings landscape continues to evolve, understanding the nuances of SEC exemptions will be crucial for businesses seeking to navigate the complex regulatory environment. By carefully evaluating the benefits and limitations of Reg A+ and Reg D, issuers can make informed decisions about their fundraising strategy and ensure compliance with SEC regulations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can retail investors buy Reg D tokens?

No. Under Regulation D Rule 506(c), the issuer must take reasonable steps to verify that every single investor is an Accredited Investor (e.g., net worth over $1M excluding primary residence).

What is the fundraising limit for Reg A+?

Under Tier 2 of Regulation A+, a company can raise up to $75 million in a 12-month period from both accredited and non-accredited investors.
