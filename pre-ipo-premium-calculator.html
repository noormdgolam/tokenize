Why a gap opens up at all

A synthetic pre-IPO contract isn't a share. Nobody holds equity, and nobody is on a cap table. It's a cash-settled bet on where a company will eventually trade, and it's available to anyone with an internet connection, at any hour, well before underwriters and regulators let conventional exchanges list the real thing. That access gap is exactly what creates the price gap: a smaller, more speculative pool of traders sets the price alone, with no supply of actual shares to correct it.

How Synthetic Prediction Markets Price Pre-IPO Companies

The concept of synthetic prediction markets involves creating a digital representation of a physical asset, such as a yet-to-be-listed company, allowing investors to trade its value in a decentralized platform. This digital representation is often referred to as a synthetic asset. By leveraging a combination of AI-driven predictive models, real-time data feeds, and community-driven trading, synthetic prediction markets can offer a highly accurate and sophisticated pricing mechanism for pre-IPO companies. One of the key advantages of synthetic prediction markets is their ability to provide a pre-IPO premium calculator. US and Global Regulatory Considerations for Synthetic Assets

Regulatory considerations for synthetic assets are a topic of significant interest in the fintech industry. As synthetic prediction markets continue to gain traction, regulators around the world are grappling with the implications of decentralized finance (DeFi) on traditional financial markets. In the United States, regulatory bodies such as the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) have expressed concerns about the risks associated with synthetic assets. While recognizing the potential benefits of decentralized finance, regulators are cautious about the lack of transparency and the potential for market manipulation. On the global front, regulatory bodies such as the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) and the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in the UK are also exploring the regulatory implications of synthetic assets. The lack of standardized regulations and guidelines in this area has created uncertainty and volatility in synthetic markets. As a result, investors must remain vigilant and conduct thorough research and due diligence before participating in synthetic prediction markets. By understanding the regulatory considerations and potential risks involved, investors can make informed decisions about their portfolios and maximize their returns. In the context of the US and other global regulatory frameworks, the use of synthetic assets for pre-IPO stocks is gaining more and more traction. In the past few years, we have witnessed an explosive growth of synthetic prediction platforms and the use of such models. With the US government being quite open to innovations around fintech, investors can expect a more stable, and clearer regulatory landscape in upcoming years.
