The Regulatory Status of Tokenized Assets in the US

By Tokenize Editorial Team • 2026-07-14

Key Takeaways

  • The SEC generally views tokenized real-world assets (RWAs) as securities, subjecting them to the strict rules of the Securities Act of 1933.
  • Most tokenized offerings in the US utilize Regulation D (506c) exemptions, restricting sales to verified accredited investors.
  • Secondary trading of these tokens cannot happen on standard crypto exchanges; it requires a registered Alternative Trading System (ATS).

As the tokenization of Real-World Assets (RWAs) accelerates, the single largest hurdle for issuers and investors is the United States regulatory environment. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has maintained a rigorous stance: simply putting an asset on a blockchain does not change its fundamental legal nature.

The Howey Test and Tokenization

The SEC determines whether an asset is a security using the Howey Test, derived from a 1946 Supreme Court case. An asset is a security if it involves an investment of money, in a common enterprise, with a reasonable expectation of profit derived from the efforts of others.

By this definition, tokenized real estate, tokenized venture capital funds, and tokenized private credit are unequivocally securities. Therefore, they cannot be legally sold to the US public without either a full SEC registration (which is incredibly costly) or a valid exemption.

Because full SEC registration is impractical for most tokenization projects, issuers rely on specific exemptions under the Securities Act of 1933. The two most prominent are:

  • Regulation D (Rule 506c): This is the most common path. It allows issuers to raise unlimited capital, but only from verified Accredited Investors. General solicitation is allowed, but the issuer must take reasonable steps to verify the investor's net worth or income.
  • Regulation A+ (Tier 2): Often called a "mini-IPO," Reg A+ allows issuers to raise up to $75 million in a 12-month period from non-accredited (retail) investors. This is the holy grail for democratization, but it requires extensive SEC qualification and ongoing financial disclosures, making it slower and more expensive to launch.

The ATS Requirement for Secondary Liquidity

The primary benefit of tokenization is liquidity, but US regulation strictly forbids trading security tokens on decentralized exchanges (DEXs) like Uniswap or standard crypto platforms like Coinbase. To comply with the law, secondary trading must occur on an Alternative Trading System (ATS) that is registered as a broker-dealer with the SEC and FINRA.

Platforms like tZERO and INX operate regulated ATS environments. When a token is issued under Reg D, it is subject to a 12-month lock-up period under Rule 144 before it can be traded on an ATS. Once the lock-up expires, the smart contract allows peer-to-peer trading, provided both the buyer and seller have passed KYC/AML checks on the ATS platform.

The regulatory status of tokenized assets in the US is complex, but the path forward is clear: compliance is not optional. Investors must ensure they are using regulated platforms, and issuers must bake compliance directly into their token smart contracts (such as using the ERC-3643 standard) to survive in the US market.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a utility token and a security token?

A utility token provides access to a product or service on a blockchain, while a security token represents a financial investment in a real-world asset (like real estate or equity) with an expectation of profit, passing the SEC's Howey Test.

What is a Regulation A+ offering?

Regulation A+ is an SEC exemption that allows companies to raise up to $75 million from both accredited and non-accredited (retail) investors, making it a popular goal for retail-focused tokenization projects.
